Changing landscape of EU international cooperation in research and innovation: What are the opportunities if ACP countries invest in their own ARD capabilities?

Cornelia E. Nauen, Mundus maris – Sciences and Arts for Sustainability asbl, Brussels, Belgium.
Sustainable improvement of human well-being depends crucially on knowledge, its production, organisation, distribution, appropriation and wise use. Access to information, the capacity to generate and use scientific and technological knowledge and human innovation give institutions and countries an edge. For ACP countries, past development efforts that ignored local circumstances, technologies and systems of knowledge wasted enormous amounts of time and resources and failed to achieve the desired result: ‘sustainable development’.

Nauen notes that ‘We are witnessing an almost incredible diversification and acceleration of knowledge production via expanded scientific and technological applications’. At the same time, there is the risk of losing some local knowledge and well-tested experiences e.g. that which is codified in indigenous knowledge systems when technological discontinuity makes it socially and sometimes practically less desirable. Nauen argues that ‘Incorporating the wealth of existing local and indigenous knowledge significantly enhances the ability to innovatively adapt to changing circumstances’. Drawing on examples of years of ACP-EU cooperation, she indicates that the rise of research and innovation in more countries diversifies the international landscape and creates new opportunities for ACP countries which are investing in their ARD capabilities and valuing their home-grown knowledge systems.

Nauen concludes that the landscape of research and scientific cooperation is changing and that while universities, research establishments and others see the need for cooperating and associating with the best in their fields from anywhere, recent policy emphasis on unspecified and broad-based competitiveness, may counter-intuitively lead to less cooperation. Yet, an ability to cooperate is what countries need for achieving competitiveness and developing solutions to the societal challenges surpassing their own means.

Read the article: knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18398.


Survey and synthesis report and lead paper on Agro-processing enterprises in the Caribbean

Prof. Neela Badrie, Deputy Dean of Research and Innovation, Faculty of Food and Agriculture, University of the West Indies, St. Augustine, Trinidad and Tobago.

This synthesis report was presented at the November 2012 event 'Adding Value to Local Foods for Food and Nutrition Security: Myth or Strategic Option'. It is based on a study that was commissioned by the Technical Centre for Agricultural and Rural Cooperation ACP-EU (CTA). The specific objectives were to survey and report on agro-processing enterprises in the Caribbean to assist in charting a three-year plan for adding value to local produce. A questionnaire was developed and distributed to agro-processing industries and responses were received from ten small, one medium and two large scale agro-enterprises. Data were obtained on, the primary raw material utilised, processing methods, types of processed products, training, food analysis and labelling, knowledge on Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point (HACCP), food regulation and legislation, marketing of the products and any recommended activities for food processing companies in the Caribbean.

Some of the recommendations for improving agro-processing were for: improved cultivar varieties availability of local raw materials and other inputs; traceability of raw materials; modern processing equipment such as automatic bottling; easy access to processing centres; adequate processing facilities, commercial settings for processing, better lay-out of processing operations and training in agro-processing; more quality analysis of products, improvement in quality standards, and financial support for meeting the expense of applying food safety management systems such as HACCP and food safety management standards such as ISO 22000 and more attractive terms in accessing finance to allow growth of small businesses.

Read the report: knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18352.

Postharvest expert meeting & policy write shop: Strengthening the engagement of knowledge institutes in ARD policy processes

Dates: 12-14 August 2013
Venue: CTA, Wageningen, The Netherlands

CTA is convinced of the need for evidence based policy and decision making in the ACP region and for building the capacity to do so. Within this context and after endorsement by senior ACP scientists at the 9th and 10th meetings of the Advisory Committee on S&T for ACP Agricultural and Rural Development in 2010 & 2011 respectively, the decision was taken to focus on analysing the ACP postharvest knowledge system as a priority issue.
In considering the elements of what constitutes the postharvest knowledge system, there is need for better understanding of the state of postharvest research, knowledge and innovation in the ACP region, the location of centres of knowledge/excellence in teaching and research; their major areas of focus, strengths and weaknesses; status of postharvest handling and processing facilities; engineering and equipment design and development capability as well as packaging facilities and capability. Given the needs, selected country case studies were conducted by focusing on one priority commodity (tracking from field to market) to learn lessons. This CTA expert consultation on postharvest knowledge systems will provide an opportunity for experts from Africa, Caribbean and the Pacific to reflect on the existing situation and design strategic options for the future.

Policy learning and planning workshop/writeshop: Improving Effectiveness of Extension and Advisory Services

Dates: 20 -23 August 2013
Venue: CTA, Wageningen, The Netherlands

CTA plans to host a Policy Learning and Planning workshop/writeshop for Improving the Effectiveness of Extension and Advisory Services. This builds on the knowledge gained from the 2011 Nairobi international conference, the results from the Extension Africa studies and other relevant knowledge sources. In November 2011, CTA in collaboration with several national, regional and international organizations hosted the International Conference on ‘Innovations in Extension and Advisory Services: Linking Knowledge to Policy and Action’ in Nairobi Kenya. The objective was to take stock of current policies, thinking and practice, successes and failures of ongoing and past reforms in extension and advisory services to specifically address meeting the future needs of small-holder farmers, marginalized communities, women and youth in a sustainable and cost effective manner.
CTA also commissioned six case studies on extension and advisory services in Benin, Jamaica, Kenya, Malawi, Nigeria and Papua New Guinea. Complementary to the ongoing initiatives, a group of scholars called the 'Africa Extension Reform Group' (AERG), in their determination to strengthen African extension in a systematic manner carried out a study entitled ‘Transforming Agricultural Extension in Africa: A Continent-Wide Study of Agricultural Extension Workers at the Grassroots’. The objective was to understand the bottlenecks and make a strong case for an alternative approach to extension programming, organization, and practice in Africa. Several experts from Africa, Caribbean and the Pacific, supported by Professor Volker Hoffmann of Germany, will participate in the CTA policy learning workshop which will serve as a platform for knowledge exchange and charting new areas for further exploration.

Agreement on the establishment of the Pacific Islands Universities Research Network (PIURN)

The Vice-Chancellors/Presidents or their nominees of eleven Pacific Islands universities at their meeting on the 9th and 10th of July 2013 at the University of the South Pacific signed an agreement confirming the establishment of the Pacific Islands Universities Research Network (PIURN). One of the goals of PIURN is to provide Science, Technology and Innovation (ST&I) policy advice at the regional and national levels. It will also seek to strengthen research content in the curriculum and in teaching, and develop shared research training initiatives on priority challenges in the Pacific region.

With a secretariat now funded by the universities themselves, work will begin on two important activities: developing a constitution, and documenting institutional research strengths. The latter priority will involve the collection of data on research in the region, and mapping and auditing of institutional research capacity. This networking initiative was identified as a priority by CTA in their 2012 work programme and supported by the Centre in 2012 and 2013. CTA’s Judith Ann Francis, Senior Programme Coordinator, Science and Technology Policy attended the July 2013 PIURN meeting and the 12 Pacific Science Inter-Congress in Fiji.

Postharvest losses of dry grains in North Western Benin

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18366

The Agriculture and Food Security Network of the Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation has conducted a quantitative survey with 105 farmers in Benin to study the storage techniques used for dry grains and the main problems encountered during storage. It investigates in more depth the issue of storage losses, their causes and means of improving food conservation in the long term. 59% of the producers of dry grains interviewed reported difficulties in bridging their household needs for dry grains until the next harvest. Producers believe just over half of the food produced by a rural household should be sufficient to prevent hunger among the rural population. Weevils (Sitophilus spp) and other insect pests are responsible for 78.5% of losses. Key issues are the poor availability of storage facilities and suitable packaging material and the lack of pest management knowledge. The document also explores management methods the country’s extension system is promoting.
(SDC Food security (PDF), 06/2013)


How the future demand for food should be met

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18386

Two recent working papers by the World Resources Institute (WRI) are the first in a new series leading to the World Resources Report 2013-14: Creating a Sustainable Food Future. One paper, ‘The great balancing act’ assesses the scope of the challenge posed by the need to feed a growing population in the near-future while reducing pressure on the environment. Priorities and solutions must focus on poverty alleviation, gender, ecosystems, climate and water, it argues. Another paper, ‘Reducing food loss and waste’, explains how efforts to reduce postharvest losses and waste perform against each of the sustainable food future criteria. It offers a thorough overview of waste and loss problems and severity and it describes how changes at the level of the value chain – some still to be researched and put into practice – can create immediate benefits to society and the environment.
(WRI, 01/07/2013)

Testing a low-tech cooling system for dairy farmers in rural Ethiopia

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18400

Lia Bardoel, a Masters Student in Industrial Design from Eindhoven University of Technology, The Netherlands, presented her work on a solar cooling system for milk and the subsequent studies she did on use of the system in remote areas. The cooling system sets out to improve the quality of evening milk and thus the income of the farmers. The solar powered system makes ice at milk collection centres, which the farmer collects and brings home during the day to cool the evening milk before bringing it to the collection point the following day. The system, developed at the Livestock and Irrigation Value chains for Ethiopian Smallholders project, is designed to work on solar and electricity grids. Contemplating the Brazilian dilemma: abundant grain and inadequate storage

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18402

Peter Goldsmith, agricultural economist at the University of Illinois, comments on the shortage of storage facilities, particularly private and cooperative, in Brazil grain producing regions. He says 'there is a 34% undercapacity of soybean storage, and the situation is aggravated by the rapidly increasing production of second-crop maize'. Using GIS software to map the coordinates of commercial, cooperative and private grain storage facilities, his research quantified the shortage in storage and identified under-served areas to help planning policy locate appropriate locations for new warehouses. The consequences of the shortage are losses and waste, mainly from the discarded grain left on the fields after harvest and from poor transportation infrastructure. The nature of non-stop, year-round farming in the tropics also contributes to the loss.
(via University of Illinois, 09/07/2013)


Global crop exposure to high temperatures in the reproductive period: historical trends and future projections

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18372

David Lobell and colleagues at the Center on Food Security and the Environment at Stanford University have analysed exposure of crops to physiologically critical temperatures in the reproductive stage, across the global harvested areas of maize, rice, soybean and wheat. For wheat, higher temperatures accelerate the crop’s physiological development, leading to earlier flowering which would reduce the plant’s exposure to extreme heat when it is at the reproductive stage. For the three other crops however, higher temperatures have little impact on the physiological development of the plants. Therefore, regardless of the impact of other global change factors (such as increasing atmospheric CO2), the researchers believe that extreme heat exposure will pose risks for global crop production. Adaptive measures such as changes in sowing dates, crop and variety switching, expansion of irrigation, and agricultural expansion into cooler areas have to be considered when drafting agricultural policy. These results show the importance of specialised data and up-to-date climate models when planning for agricultural adaptation.
(Center on Food Security and the Environment, 14/06/2013)

Farmer’s choice of seeds in four EU countries under different levels of GM crop adoption

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18373

Researcher Angelika Hilbeck at the Institute of Integrative Biology of the Swiss Federal Institute of Technology looked into the agricultural seed catalogues available to farmers in countries with different degrees of GM crop adoption (Austria, Germany, Spain, and Switzerland). She and her colleagues estimated how much real-world choice in seeds maize farmers have in each of the countries. The results show that, between 1995 and 2011, in the countries that did not adopt GM crop (Austria, Germany and Switzerland), actual restrictions and regulations of GM crops have not decreased seed choices/cultivar availability for farmers. In contrast, they observed that in Spain, which has adopted GM maize, the seed market was more concentrated with fewer differentiated cultivars on offer: the overall number of maize cultivars declined. The research also plotted the yields over the time period only to note that there was no reduction in yields in non-adopting countries.
(Environmental Sciences Europe 2013, 25:12 doi:10.1186/2190-4715-25-12)

The global hidden hunger indices and maps: an advocacy tool for action

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18371

Indices and maps of global hidden hunger are being developed to help prioritise programme assistance and to serve as an evidence-based global advocacy tool. In this research paper, Sumithra Muthayya of Sax Institute Sydney, Australia together with specialists from both the non-profit and for-profit nutrition sectors, creates two types of hidden hunger indices and maps (using data on stunting, anaemia, retinol levels and estimates of Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) attributed to micronutrient deficiencies). Of note, occurrence of iodine deficiency did not conform to that of other micronutrients (it mainly occurs in developed countries). The study demonstrates how these indices and maps provide crucial data for tackling global multiple micronutrient deficiencies and could help to scale up effective nutrition interventions.
(PLOS ONE 8(6): e67860. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0067860 June 2013)


Tomato fruits: a good target for iodine biofortification

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18370

Iodine deficiency affects two billion people worldwide. This experiment conducted by the PlantLab, Italy and the private company SQM S.A., Chile evaluated the possibility of biofortifying tomato fruits with iodine. Results showed that the use of iodised salts induced a significant increase in the fruit’s iodine content in doses that did not affect plant growth and development. This suggests that the tomato is a particularly suitable crop for iodine biofortification programmes. The pattern and magnitude of iodine deficiency does not conform to that of other micronutrients. The greatest proportions of children with iodine deficiency are in the Eastern Mediterranean (46.6%), European (44.2%), and African (40.4%) regions. The daily Dietary Reference Intake (USA) for iodine is between 110 µg for infants and 290 µg for lactating mothers. Some foods are naturally rich in iodine: beans, potatoes (with skin), gray (rock) salt, sea vegetables (kelp), and yoghurt.
(Front. Plant Sci., 4:205. doi: 10.3389/fpls.2013.00205; 27/06/2013)


Mining the wealth of the world's seed banks

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18389

In a recent Comment in Nature, Cornell University plant geneticist Susan McCouch and colleagues says that the biodiversity stored in plant gene banks coupled with advances in genetics and plant breeding may hold the keys for meeting the challenge of climate change, soil degradation and water and land shortages. Though seeds are readily accessible in 1,700 gene banks, it is difficult for breeders to make use of the wealth of genetic material in seed banks because of a lack of information about the genes in most plants and the traits they confer. The paper outlines a plan to address these constraints: 1) a massive genetic sequencing effort on seed-bank holdings; 2) a broad phenotyping initiative; 3) an internationally accessible informatics infrastructure.
(Eurek Alert, 01/05/2013)


Genetics, genomics and gene modification: What does the future of animal production hold?

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18401

Professor David Hume, Director of The Roslin Institute at the Universty of Edinburgh, UK looks at the challenge to meet the demand for animal products whilst at the same time reducing the overall environmental impact. According to Hume, it is very unlikely that existing genetic variation in animals will continue to generate the rate of gain in size or offspring number, for example, obtained in the past. So a different kind of genetically-modified animal will be required. Hume believes that ‘transgenesis’ (genetic engineering) to produce desired traits is possible and could provide clear benefits to the consumer in terms of cost, health, animal welfare and environment if regulatory requirements were based upon science rather than assertion. He concludes that sustainable gains in productivity of livestock will only be achieved if governments recognise that the required research is a ‘public good’, and re-enter and re-engage with the livestock research sector with substantial investment.
(BBSRC, 08/07/2013)


Breeding better barley for Ethiopia's staple bread

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18390

A team of plant breeders led by Fetien Abay of Mekelle University in Ethiopia and Åsmund Bjørnstad of the Norwegian University of Life Sciences followed an approach called participatory plant breeding to select the varieties of barley most adapted for the preparation of injera (a very popular bread in Ethiopia). They found that, if cooked in the traditional way, injera prepared with barley from a variety that is a cross between the Himblil and Saesa variety, scored very high among the Ethiopian tasters.

Editors’comments: Production of Teff, one of the earliest plants to be domesticated and the grain of which is traditionally used in Ethiopia for the preparation of injera, while no disaggregated stats exists, is almost two times that of barley in Ethiopia (FAO stats, HarvestChoice). It is also high in dietary fibre, iron, and provides calcium and protein. A question for scientists/breeders and policymakers to ponder: While the cheaper injera made from barley is comparable taste wise, how does it compare on nutritional content? Another issue is that teff is adapted to numerous environments; drought stressed and water logged. Are the barley varieties similarly adapted?
(Crop Science Society of America, 02/07/2013)

Five Pacific island countries to revive and unlock the potential of bele, an indigenous vegetable

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18391

Papua New Guinea, New Caledonia, Vanuatu, Solomon Islands and Fiji will address together priority research needs to revive bele (Abelmoschus manihot, also known as aibika or slippery cabbage) a neglected but nutritious native crop. The project activities aim at rationalising national bele collections, establishing regional collections and documenting traditional knowledge both on farming practices and food processing. Bele is known to have medicinal properties, requires low maintenance, and some varieties are tolerant to drought, other to pests. Unfortunately, some 30 varieties of bele were noted to have been lost over the years in Papua New Guinea in the early 1990s.

Results and innovations from transnational research partnerships

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18376

Presentations by key staff of participating organisations on topics such as ‘Getting research and innovation into policy and practice’ can be downloaded.
In his presentation, François Münger, Head of the Global Programme on Water Initiatives at SDC, details the barriers to linking research, policy and practice, such as: Low recognition and rating of practice and policy influence in an academic cursus; Difference of timing between research and political rhythms which limits the possibility to take advantage of the political opportunities; Results often presented to potential users at the end of research project. According to him, trans-disciplinary and multi-stakeholder approaches can further innovation by connecting researchers to larger networks or introduce them to trans-disciplinary approaches that can help them think out of the box. Scientific development in African countries

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18383

This report by the International Network for the Availability of Scientific Publications (INASP) presents findings from a bibliometric analysis of the 26 most productive African countries in Scopus (the world's largest abstract and citation database of peer-reviewed literature) between 1996 and 2009. Changes in the scientific research output and comparisons with global patterns across 27 scientific knowledge domains (8 of which can be directly related to the agricultural research). It details publication patterns over ten years, the number of research papers produced per year per country, the nature of international collaboration in multi-author publications, etc. Strengths and weakness are identified which can be used to inform future decision-making processes. Although Africa’s contribution to the world of scientific publications remains low, the results indicate that the number of scientific publications is growing while international corporation remains almost the same.
(INASP, 20/06/2013)

Interplanting small-scale timber plantations with native agroforestry species in Solomon

knowledge.cta.int/en/content/view/full/18399

This project coordinated by the Australian Centre for International Agricultural Research (ACIAR) shows that village-owned plantations of high-value timber trees, mainly teak (Tectona grandis) and mahogany (Swietenia macrophylla) in the Solomon Islands can benefit from interplanting with the indigenous tree Flueggea flexuosa and other tree species native to the Pacific region. The rationale behind this interplanting is that commercial plantations must always be thinned and removing young timbers trees that cannot be sold offers no practical advantages. However, thinning the plantations by only removing indigenous tree species can benefit the villagers in many ways: with fruits and other tree products, firewood, and construction materials.
(ACIAR, 26/06/2013)
